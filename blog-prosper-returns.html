My 5-Year Experience with Prosper P2P Investing

August 23, 2025

About five years ago, I decided to allocate some capital into peer-to-peer lending through Prosper. At the time, the platform’s advertised interest rates looked very attractive—often in the 8–12% range depending on loan grades. It seemed like a promising way to diversify outside of stocks and real estate.

Over time, the reality turned out to be different. Defaults, late payments, servicing fees, and early repayments slowly chipped away at my returns. As you can see in the chart below, my rate of return started strong, peaked near double digits, but then gradually declined as loans aged and more defaults occurred.

In the end, my all-time return settled around 3.8%—much lower than the advertised yields. This doesn’t mean Prosper is a bad platform, but it highlights the gap between headline rates and actual net returns once you factor in defaults, idle cash, and taxes.

Takeaways

  • The headline borrower rates are not what investors actually earn.
  • Defaults and servicing fees have the biggest impact on reducing returns.
  • Prepayments limit interest earned and create reinvestment risk.
  • After taxes, the final return can be closer to a savings account than a stock market investment.

Overall, I view Prosper as a decent experiment, but not the wealth-building engine it once seemed. A steady 3–4% return is respectable compared to a bank account, but it doesn’t justify the extra risk for me personally.
